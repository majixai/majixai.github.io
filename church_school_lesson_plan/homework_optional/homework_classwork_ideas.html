Optional Homework & Classwork Ideas (6-7 Year Olds)

Guiding Principles for Optional Homework & Classwork:

  • Reinforce, Don't Introduce: Activities should reinforce concepts already taught in class.
  • Age-Appropriate: Tasks should be manageable for a 6-7 year old with minimal adult help.
  • Engaging & Fun: Make it enjoyable, not a burden.
  • Optional & Flexible: Emphasize that homework is optional to avoid pressure on families. Provide choices when possible.
  • Family Connection: Some homework can encourage family discussion or participation.
  • Brief: Homework should not take a long time (e.g., 10-15 minutes).

I. Optional Homework Ideas:

These can be sent home weekly or bi-weekly, perhaps with a small note explaining the concept covered in class.

Bible Story & Values Reinforcement:

  1. Drawing Response:
    • "Draw a picture of your favorite part of the Bible story we learned today: [Story Name]."
    • "Draw a picture showing someone being kind (or another value like sharing, forgiving)."
  2. Story Retell Prompt:
    • "Tell a family member about the story of [Bible Character] we learned today."
    • (Optional) "Ask your family member what their favorite part of the story is."
  3. Memory Verse Practice:
    • "Practice our memory verse: [Verse Text]. Try saying it to a family member!"
    • Provide a small card with the verse written or drawn.
  4. "I Wonder" Question:
    • "This week we learned about [Topic]. Talk with your family: What is one thing you wonder about God or the Bible?" (Parents can help write it down if the child wants to share it next class).
  5. Value Spotting:
    • "Look for ways people show [Christian Value, e.g., helpfulness] at home or in your neighborhood this week. Be ready to share one example if you want to."
  6. Simple Act of Kindness:
    • "With your family's help, think of one small act of kindness you can do for someone this week (e.g., help set the table, make a card for a grandparent)."

Literacy & Numeracy Reinforcement (If part of the curriculum):

  1. Sight Word Hunt:
    • "Look for these sight words we learned ([list 2-3 words]) in a book you read with your family."
  2. Letter/Sound Practice:
    • "Find three things in your house that start with the letter /S/ sound." Draw them or tell someone.
  3. Counting Fun:
    • "Count how many [e.g., windows, spoons, blue things] are in your kitchen."
  4. Story Creation (Oral or Drawn):
    • "Make up a short, happy story. You can tell it to someone or draw pictures for it."

II. Optional Classwork Ideas (for early finishers, centers, or choice time):

These are activities children can do in class if they finish other work early, or during a designated activity/center time.

Bible & Values Focus:

  1. Bible Story Sequencing Pictures: Provide 3-4 simple pictures depicting scenes from a recent Bible story. Children put them in the correct order.
  2. Value Sorting Cards: Simple scenario cards (e.g., "Sharing toys," "Pushing someone"). Children sort them into "Shows God's Love" / "Doesn't Show God's Love" piles.
  3. Prayer Prompts Jar: Write simple prayer prompts on slips of paper (e.g., "Someone I can pray for," "Something I'm thankful for"). Children can pick one and draw or write a prayer.
  4. Bible Character Matching: Pictures of Bible characters and names or key items associated with them (e.g., David - Harp/Slingshot; Noah - Ark).
  5. Stained Glass Window Coloring Pages: Coloring pages with Christian symbols or simple Bible scenes.

Creative & General Learning:

  1. Free Drawing/Writing Table: Paper, crayons, markers, pencils available for children to draw or write about what they've learned or what's on their mind.
  2. Playdough Sculpting: Relate to the theme (e.g., "Sculpt an animal from Noah's Ark," "Sculpt something God created").
  3. Building Blocks Challenge: "Build a church," "Build a home for a Bible character."
  4. Puzzles & Manipulatives: Age-appropriate puzzles (Bible-themed if available), pattern blocks, linking cubes.
  5. Quiet Reading Corner: A selection of age-appropriate Christian storybooks or picture books.

Note on Tracking/Sharing Optional Work:

  • If children bring back optional homework, have a simple way to acknowledge it (e.g., a sticker, a quick positive comment, a designated sharing time for those who wish to share).
  • The goal is encouragement and reinforcement, not grading.
